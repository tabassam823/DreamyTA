Abstract
A number of writers have modelled imperfect markets using games in
which the strategies are supply functions, that is, mappings from prices
to quantities produced. Two representations of this problem have been
analyzed, which may be referred to as ex ante and ex post, depending
on whether strategies are chosen before or after demand shocks are observed. In this paper, we examine the relationship between equilibria in
supply curves derived using the ex ante and ex post approaches. We derive conditions under which a linear ex ante solution coincides with the
ex post solution. These conditions generalize the case of linear demand
and quadratic cost, analyzed by Klemperer and Meyer. We demonstrate
that all ex ante solutions derived in this way are unique.

Introduction

Beginning with Grossman (1981) and Robson (1981), a number of writers (Klemperer and Meyer 1989; Turnbull 1983) have modelled imperfect markets using
games in which the strategies are supply functions, that is, mappings from prices
to quantities produced. In the single-product, homogenous goods case, market
outcomes may be represented by supply curves, that is, one-dimensional manifolds in price-quantity space.
The main di¢ culty with modelling competition in supply functions is the
multiplicity of equilibria. As shown by Klemperer and Meyer (1989), in the
absence of restrictions on the set of supply functions allowed as strategies, any
individually rational market outcome may be derived as a Nash equilibrium.
Two solutions to this problem have been put forward, which may be referred to as ex ante and ex post. Both solutions involve demand uncertainty,
which is necessary to observe movements along the supply curve. Uncertainty
is represented by an additive demand shock ":

Conclusion

The graphical representation of competitive markets in terms of supply and
demand curves has proved a powerful and fruitful approach to economic analysis.
Using the ex ante and ex post equilibrium concepts presented above, it is possible
to extend this approach to the case of oligopoly. In this paper, we have shown
that the ex ante and ex post solutions are closely related. We have generalized
the uniqueness results previously derived for the ex ante case by Klemperer and
Meyer.
